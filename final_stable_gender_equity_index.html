Gender at Work

The illusion of workplace equality

Diversity policies, flexible work arrangements, and leadership programs often present workplaces as progressive and inclusive. Yet many workers remain outside these systems entirely.

Who actually has access to these systems?

Flexible work?

Support systems often look inclusive from inside stable organisations.

Who is missing?

Casual, part-time and insecure workers are often less visible.

Behind the language of inclusion exists another reality — insecure, unstable and invisible forms of labour.

Hidden reality

Precarious work exists outside formal support.

Precarious workers are often employed through insecure, casual, part-time or unstable arrangements. Many exist outside formal organisational systems, with reduced access to career progression, internal communication, workplace support and institutional visibility.

Why it is gendered

Workplaces reward uninterrupted labour.

Women are disproportionately pushed into insecure employment through unpaid care responsibilities and expectations around flexibility. Flexible work may appear available in policy, but it is not always accessible in practice.

Structural exclusion

Many equality initiatives rely on HR systems, internal communication and employer partnerships. This can unintentionally exclude precarious workers who are less connected to formal workplace structures.

The workers most affected by inequality are often the least likely to access support.

Our Process

1

Research

We examined academic studies, feminist economic research and workplace reports on precarious labour, workplace inequality and gendered emotional burden.

2

Analysis

We explored how workplace systems unintentionally exclude precarious workers through visibility gaps, inaccessible support structures and employer-focused equality models.

3

Intervention

Our intervention uses infographic-based communication and digital storytelling to improve accessibility, awareness and engagement for workers outside formal workplace systems.

Grace Papers

Existing systems already attempt to improve workplace equality.

Grace Papers supports workplace equality through coaching, flexible work planning and organisational change. However, because its model works strongly through employer partnerships, workers outside stable organisational systems may be less likely to access this support.

Human impact

Precarious work is not only an economic issue. It affects emotional wellbeing, mental health, stress, stability and the everyday burden of balancing paid labour with unpaid care responsibilities.
