GENDER AT WORK

The illusion of workplace equality

Diversity policies, flexible work arrangements, and leadership initiatives often present workplaces as progressive and inclusive. Yet many workers remain outside these systems entirely.

Flexible Work
Diversity Policy

Behind the language of inclusion exists another reality — insecure, unstable, and invisible forms of labour.

PRECARIOUS WORK

Casual.
Part-time.
Unstable.

Precarious workers often exist outside formal organisational systems. Their work is insecure, irregular, and frequently excluded from workplace support structures. Many workers in these roles have limited access to career progression, workplace communication, or institutional support.

WHY IS IT GENDERED?

Workplace systems reward uninterrupted labour.

Women are disproportionately pushed into precarious employment due to unpaid care responsibilities and workplace expectations surrounding flexibility. Flexible work often exists in theory but not in practice.

Structural Exclusion

Many workplace equality initiatives rely on HR systems, employer partnerships, and internal communication structures. However, precarious workers are often the least connected to these systems.

“The workers most affected by inequality are often the least likely to access support.”

Our Process

1

Research

We examined academic studies, feminist economic research, and workplace reports surrounding precarious labour and workplace inequality.

2

Analysis

We explored how workplace systems unintentionally exclude precarious workers through visibility gaps, inaccessible support structures, and employer-focused models.

3

Intervention

Our intervention focuses on communication accessibility and visibility through digital storytelling and infographic-based engagement.

GRACE PAPERS

Existing systems already attempt to improve workplace equality.

Grace Papers works with organisations to support flexible work, leadership development, and workplace inclusion. However, their employer-based model may unintentionally limit accessibility for workers outside stable organisational systems.

The human impact of precarious work

Beyond economics, precarious work affects emotional wellbeing, mental health, stress levels, and long-term stability. Many women experience the simultaneous burden of paid labour and unpaid care work.

Intervention Methods

Visual Storytelling

Creating emotional accessibility through visual communication and feminist digital design.

Infographic Intervention

Simplifying academic and workplace research into accessible communication formats.

Accessibility

Designing communication systems that reach workers outside formal workplace structures.

Workplace equality must be visible, accessible, and inclusive for all workers — not only those already inside stable organisational systems.
